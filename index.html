§ 1 · A cloud with a secret

What shape is this data?

Here are twenty-four measurements. Maybe they are two features of some cells, or the readings of two sensors. Your eye needs about a tenth of a second to announce the important thing about them: these points sit on a loop.

Now try asking statistics — the buttons are below.

The average lands in the one place where there is no data — it sits in the hole it cannot see. The trend line shrugs; there is no trend. Means, variances, and regressions summarize where the data is. None of them can say the sentence “there is a hole in the middle.”

That sentence is a statement about shape, and the branch of math that makes it precise is topology. Persistent homology is topology turned into something you can run on a matrix — which means you, knowing linear algebra, already own most of the machinery. The rest of this tour builds it in front of you.

Why expect data to have a shape at all? Because measurements are usually many, but the dials behind them are few. A face photographed under a turning light is a million pixels, yet only one angle truly varies — so those million-dimensional points sweep out a one-dimensional curve. This is the manifold hypothesis: high-dimensional data tends to lie near a low-dimensional shape, because the process that generated it had only a few degrees of freedom. Anything periodic — an angle, a phase, a cycle in a chemical oscillator — traces a loop. Shapes are not a curiosity in data; they are what generating processes look like from the outside.

Everything here happens in two dimensions so that you can watch it. Nothing in the mathematics will care: the algorithm we build only ever reads pairwise distances between points, and points in ℝ1000 have those too.

§ 2 · Inflate everything

A point has no shape. Give it some.

A finite set of points is topologically boring — it is just dust. The classic trick: grow a disk of radius r around every point, and study the union of disks instead. Drag the slider at the bottom.

Small r: dust. Large r: one featureless blob. Somewhere in between, the union of disks briefly becomes the shape your eye saw — a ring with a hole in it.

So which radius is the right one? Here is the idea that makes the whole subject work: refuse to choose. Sweep r from zero to huge, and keep a diary of every topological event along the way — the moment each feature appears, and the moment it disappears. Features that survive a long stretch of the sweep are real; features that flicker in and out are noise.

That diary is called a barcode. Let’s record one.

§ 3 · Births and deaths

Chapter one: islands merging

The simplest kind of feature is a connected piece. At r = 0 every point is its own island — that is a birth, all at once, for every point. As the disks grow, islands touch and merge. Each merge ends one island’s independent story: a death. Draw each lifespan as a horizontal bar and you get the barcode of H0 (“H-zero” — the counter of connected pieces), lower right.

The vertical line sweeps with the slider: bars to its left-and-under are alive right now. Hover a bar to light up the island whose life it records.

Two things to notice. The short bars die young — they are points that quickly joined a neighbor; that is the texture of noise. The two long bars are the two genuine clusters, and they stay separate until the disks are large enough to bridge the gap between clusters. And one bar never dies: at the end everything is a single blob, and that last component lives forever. Its bar runs off the edge of the chart.

§ 4 · Loops

Chapter two: holes

Components merge; that story only ever loses characters. The more interesting characters appear along the way: loops. A loop is born at the radius where a ring of disks first closes, and it dies at the radius where its middle fills in. Its bar lives in the barcode of H1, the counter of holes.

Hover the long bar and the loop itself lights up in the cloud — that bar is that hole, drawn as a lifespan. Switch to the figure eight: two holes, two long bars. Switch to random scatter: little loops flicker into existence by accident and are filled almost immediately — all short bars, no story.

This is the entire reading lesson: long bars are features of the data; short bars are accidents of the sampling.

§ 5 · Trust

Why you may believe a barcode

A summary you cannot trust under noise is a horoscope. So: shake the data and watch what the barcode does.

The rubble at the bottom churns with every shake — but the long H1 bar barely trembles. This robustness is not luck; it is a theorem (the stability theorem): perturb the points a little, and no bar’s endpoints move by more than a little. Statistics from topology come with a warranty.

The diagram view is the same information drawn differently: each bar becomes one dot, at (birth, death). Everything sits above the diagonal — you cannot die before you are born — and distance above the diagonal is lifespan. Noise hugs the diagonal; features float high above it. Topologists keep both pictures; the diagram scales better when there are thousands of bars.

Circles for H0, triangles for H1 — the shapes, not just the colors, tell the two families apart.

§ 6 · Discretize

Triangles, not disks

A computer cannot push disks around the plane; it needs the picture rebuilt from finitely many parts. The recipe, called the Vietoris–Rips complex, is one sentence long: connect two points with an edge when their disks overlap; fill in a triangle when all three of its edges are present. Drag the points and watch the rule fire.

Points, edges, triangles (and in higher dimensions, their big siblings) are called simplices, and a pile of them glued compatibly is a simplicial complex. The growing family of complexes you get by sweeping r — each one containing the last — is a filtration. That is the object persistent homology actually eats.

Fine print: the true union-of-disks picture is a slightly different complex (the Čech complex, which asks for a common overlap of all three disks). Rips is its cheaper cousin — it only ever looks at pairwise distances — and for intuition the two rhyme. Every software package you will meet computes Rips.

Pause on what just quietly happened: the recipe never mentioned the plane. It asks one question — how far apart is this pair of points? — and builds everything from the answers. Hand it distances between gene-expression profiles, between frames of a video, between configurations of a protein, and it builds the same complexes and, soon, the same barcodes. This is how the manifold hypothesis becomes testable: if your thousand-dimensional data really does live on a loop, the loop will show up as a long bar — no plot required, no projection to 2-D, no luck.

§ 7 · Where the linear algebra lives

How a matrix notices a hole

Here is the one genuinely clever move in homology, and it is a move you already know. Work over ℤ/2 — the arithmetic where 1 + 1 = 0. Then a set of edges is the same thing as a vector: one coordinate per edge, a 1 where the edge is in the set. Sets of edges become vectors; “combine two sets” becomes addition; and every question becomes linear algebra.

Define one linear map, the boundary ∂: it sends each edge to its pair of endpoint vertices. Because 1 + 1 = 0, when you take the boundary of a path, all the interior vertices cancel in pairs, and only the two ends survive. Click edges to build a chain and watch its boundary.

∂c = 0 — select some edges.

Try to build a nonzero chain whose boundary is zero. You will discover — with your hands — that the only way to make every endpoint cancel is to close the path into a loop. Chains with ∂c = 0 are called cycles, and you have just found that cycles = the null space of the boundary matrix. A hole-detector, hiding in a kernel.

One repair is needed. Select the three edges around the shaded triangle: a cycle — but it encircles nothing; the triangle is filled. Filled-in cycles are exactly the boundaries of triangle-chains, i.e. the column space of the next boundary map ∂2. Those should count for nothing. So we declare two cycles equivalent when they differ by boundaries, and define

H1 = null(∂1) / im(∂2) — cycles, modulo filled-in cycles.

The dimension of that quotient counts independent holes (it’s called a Betti number). By rank–nullity you can already compute it: β1 = (#edges − rank ∂1) − rank ∂2. Two ranks. That is all homology is.

In textbooks this construction runs through group theory — chains form abelian groups and H1 is a quotient group. Over ℤ/2 the groups happen to be vector spaces, so every tool you own applies. You are not getting a watered-down version; ℤ/2 is what actual software uses.

§ 8 · The algorithm

The whole subject is one matrix reduction

Now put the sweep (§2) and the algebra (§7) together. List every simplex of the filtration — vertices, edges, triangles — in order of arrival, and build one big boundary matrix D: column j holds the boundary of the j-th simplex. Then reduce it left to right, like Gaussian elimination with one house rule: you may only add columns from the past (and you keep going until each column’s lowest 1 is in a row no earlier column claims).

Each column ends one of two ways, and both ways mean something:

— the column becomes zero: this simplex closed a cycle. A feature is born.
— the column keeps a lowest 1 in row i: this simplex fills the cycle that simplex i created. The feature born at i dies now. Pair them; that pair is a bar.

Four points, fourteen simplices. Step through it with the buttons below.

Press step to process the first column.

Watch for three moments. When the fourth side of the square arrives, its column reduces to zero — the loop is born. When the first triangle arrives, it dies in the same instant its diagonal creates a smaller loop: a bar of length zero, which the barcode silently drops. And when the second triangle arrives, the visible loop dies — the one bar you saw in §4 falls out of arithmetic. The very last column also reduces to zero: a class in H2, the next floor of the theory. Same matrix, same rules, no new ideas.

That is persistent homology, all of it: order simplices by arrival, reduce one boundary matrix, read the pairs. Everything you hovered over earlier in this tour was computed exactly this way, live, in your browser.

Coda

Why does anyone do this? Because “how many components, and how many holes, at which scales” turns out to be a genuinely useful summary of real data: the branching of blood vessels, pores in a material, the cosmic web, protein structures, the activity of neural populations. Persistent homology is the rare exported good of pure mathematics — a quotient of a null space, shipped to a biology lab, with a stability theorem for a warranty.

To go further: Robert Ghrist’s essay Barcodes: the persistent topology of data is the classic next step; Edelsbrunner & Harer’s Computational Topology is the standard textbook; and Ripser is what the professionals use to reduce those matrices very, very fast. For the tools I use to point this at language-model internals, see the blog-post version of this tour.

Every barcode here was computed in your browser by a few hundred lines of vanilla JavaScript doing ℤ/2 column reduction — the same algorithm you stepped through in §8, just with no one watching.
